Journey Narrative

The last inhabited valley on earth

"I had been warned about the cold. I had read the temperature records. I had bought the right gear. None of it prepared me for what −18°C feels like when you are holding a camera at 4,400 metres before sunrise."

Spiti in winter is a valley of extraordinary contrasts. The landscape is stripped bare — the monsoon floods, the summer trekkers, the tourist buses are all gone. What remains is the essential: brown mountains, white snow, a frozen river that glints like hammered tin, and the architecture of a civilisation that learned centuries ago how to survive here.

"In the silence of Spiti, you hear things that no city has ever let you notice — the wind on stone, your own breath, the absolute absence of everything else."

I stayed for 23 days across four villages — Kaza, Langza, Key, and the extraordinary Chicham. My camera battery lasted exactly 18 minutes in the coldest mornings. I started carrying three batteries in my inner jacket pocket, switching them out against my body heat.

This collection moves through a single emotion: the feeling of being very small in something very ancient and very cold, and finding that feeling unexpectedly beautiful.

Behind the Shot

Frames made in extreme cold

01

Key Monastery at −16°C

"I had walked 3km in the dark to be at Key Monastery before first light. The camera battery died 4 minutes after I arrived. I switched to the one from my jacket pocket, fingers working through gloves. By the time the light hit the monastery walls, I had exactly nine frames before that battery also died. This is frame seven."

02

The River That Forgot How to Move

"The Spiti River was completely frozen for a 40-kilometre stretch. I walked onto the ice to compose this frame — which in retrospect was extremely unwise. The ice was groaning. I heard it crack somewhere downstream. I made three exposures and walked back very slowly. The light lasted another 25 minutes. I watched it from the bank."
